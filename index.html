The quarterback is the most important position in all of sports. As the game of football becomes increasingly pass heavy, this fact becomes more and more true year in and year out. In fact, between the 32 NFL teams in 2022, there is currently \$3,084,928,180 invested in quarterbacks (NFL active player contracts.). This number will only rise as the rookie quarterbacks drafted in 2022 sign their contracts. Furthermore, it recently became rumored that Tom Brady will sign a \$375 million contract to go into broadcasting once his NFL career is done. That is, because of his impact on the field as a quarterback, Brady will be paid nearly $40 million a year to work roughly 6 hours a day, just 3 days a week, for about half of the weeks in a year.

That being said, with great finance comes great responsibility. Quarterbacks, as well as the teams and management staffs that select them in the draft are constantly judged for their decisions. If a general manager and coach use a highly valued draft pick on a quarterback who does not pan out, the careers of all three will be over in a heartbeat. Take the Cleveland Browns for example. Since 2000, They have had 31 quarterbacks start for them (Wikimedia Foundation. (2022, April 25). List of Cleveland Browns starting quarterbacks.). In that same time period, they have had 10 head coaches (Organization), and 11 general managers (Cleveland Browns. The Pittsburgh steelers, on the other hand, have had just 11 quarterbacks start for them in that timeframe (Wikimedia Foundation. (2022, May 4). List of Pittsburgh Steelers starting quarterbacks.). As a result of the stability, they have had just 2 coaches (Pittsburgh) and 1 general manager (Wikimedia Foundation. (2022, May 9). Kevin Colbert.) since 2000.

As you may imagine, the connection between quarterback success and job security often causes franticness amongst the coaches and general managers who are uncertain in their starting quarterbacks abilities. As a result, many people, including general managers, coaches, analysts, commentators, fans, and even quarterbacks themselves, have tried to answer the age old question: How do you draft a good quarterback?

Many theories have arisen in response to this question. In this analysis, I will take a look at a few different claims in regard to drafting a quarter back, analyze their validity, and attempt to predict the future success of some of the leagues best and brightest young quarterback talent.

Theory 1: If I was running a team, heres my rule for the GM: There are only 2 spots where youre allowed to draft a quarterback. In the top 10, or [in the] 6th round or later. Nick Wright, Whats Wright? With Nick Wright (Wright)

Theory 2: Fast QBs lead to more success Everyone on Twitter in regard to Malik Willis, Lamar Jackson, etc.

Theory 3: Short QBs will struggle to win Claim made yearly about shorter quarterbacks such as Baker Mayfield, Kyler Murray, Drew Brees, etc.

Theory 4: If you're going to put all of your eggs in one quarterback's basket and nowadays, you don't have much of a choice then you might have to be willing to make some schematic changes in order to support him. Brad Gagnon, Bleacher Report (Gagnon)

Theory 5: (In regard to spending extra draft capital on offense in order to improve a young quarterbacks play) My goal in this draft is to make Daniel Jones successful. If that means that Garrett Wilson is my wide receiver 4, thats gonna help him be successful. Brett Kollmann, (Kollmann), (21:52)

Now that we have all of our data mapped out, let's take a look back at the hypothesies.

  • Theory 1: If I was running a team, heres my rule for the GM: There are only 2 spots where youre allowed to draft a quarterback. In the top 10, or [in the] 6th round or later. Nick Wright, Whats Wright? With Nick Wright (Wright)

  • Theory 2: Fast QBs lead to more success Everyone on Twitter in regard to Malik Willis, Lamar Jackson, etc.

  • Theory 3: Short QBs will struggle to win Claim made yearly about shorter quarterbacks such as Baker Mayfield, Kyler Murray, Drew Brees, etc.

  • Theory 4: If you're going to put all of your eggs in one quarterback's basket and nowadays, you don't have much of a choice then you might have to be willing to make some schematic changes in order to support him. Brad Gagnon, Bleacher Report (Gagnon)

  • Theory 5: (In regard to spending extra draft capital on offense in order to improve a young quarterbacks play) My goal in this draft is to make Daniel Jones successful. If that means that Garrett Wilson is my wide receiver 4, thats gonna help him be successful. Brett Kollmann, (Kollmann), (21:52)

Looking at charts 1a, 1b, 3a, and 3b, we can reject Theory 1. While there are great quarterbacks selected in the top 10, and in the 6th round or later, Wright's theory totally discounts all the great quarterbacks picked in between.That being said, the hottest areas for good quarterbacks appear to be in the 1st round and on to the early 2nd.

We can then accept Theory 2. While it may not be necessary to have a quarterback as fast as Lamar Jackson or Malike Willis (who's 40 yd times are estimated at 4.34 and 4.40 respectively), charts 4a and 4b show that it certainly does help to have a faster-than-average quarterback at the helm.

Theory 3 can be rejected. Chart 5a shows an even number of quarterbacks in regard to being over or under the average height for the position amongst those who won the Super Bowl. Chart 5b shows a nearly even split amongst quarterbacks who appeared in a superbowl

Theory 4 can be accepted. The final 6 charts show a major change in draft capital investment after drafting a franchise quarterback in an attempt to boost the offense around them.

Finally, in a similar manner to Theory 4, Theory 5 can also be accepted.
